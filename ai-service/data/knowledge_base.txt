Grounding can help when a person feels overwhelmed. Encourage them to notice five things they can see, four they can feel, three they can hear, two they can smell, and one they can taste. This lowers intensity by reconnecting attention to the present moment.

When stress is high, smaller tasks are safer than ambitious plans. Suggest breaking the next step into something that can be done in under ten minutes so the person regains a sense of control instead of feeling trapped by the size of the problem.

Slow breathing is most effective when the exhale is longer than the inhale. A simple pattern is to inhale for four seconds and exhale for six seconds for five rounds, especially when the body feels tense or restless.

If sadness is lingering, encourage gentle connection rather than isolation. Reaching out to one trusted person, even with a short honest message, can reduce the burden of carrying everything alone.

Low energy often improves when a person protects recovery instead of forcing peak performance. Recommend hydration, lighter expectations, and one restorative routine such as an earlier wind-down, a short walk, or a screen break.

Journaling works best when it stays simple. One helpful structure is: what feels heavy, what is outside my control, what small step is still possible today. This can reduce mental noise and make the next action clearer.
